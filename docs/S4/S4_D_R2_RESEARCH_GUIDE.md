# S4-D-R2 Research Guide

## Research home

- Sphere: `S4`
- Lane: `S4-D Cross-Domain Mechanism Models`
- Research line: `S4-D-R2 Shared Mechanism Interpretation`

## Why this study belongs here

`S4-D-R1` identifies transferable signatures. `S4-D-R2` asks which recurring motifs are truly useful across contexts and which are only superficial similarity.

This makes `S4-D` a cross-domain reasoning layer, not a collection of analogies.

## Core question

Which biochemical response motifs recur across domains strongly enough to support shared mechanism interpretation, and where should transfer stop?

## Practical substudies

### `S4-D-R2a` Identify Recurring Response Motifs

Goal:

- identify motifs that appear across disease, plant stress, environmental stress, and model systems

Focus:

- oxidative stress
- lipid remodeling
- amino-acid metabolism
- energy metabolism
- inflammatory or defense-linked metabolism

### `S4-D-R2b` Rank Shared Perturbation Logic Across Contexts

Goal:

- rank shared motifs by transferability and context limits

Focus:

- shared vs context-specific direction
- biological system type
- mechanism depth
- validation burden
- risk of false universalism

## Data classes to collect

- motif label
- domain or organism context
- condition or stressor
- metabolite or pathway support
- shared direction
- context-specific caveat
- transferability score
- evidence source

## Required outputs

- shared motif table
- transferability ranking
- context-boundary notes
- cross-domain interpretation report

## Claim discipline

At this stage we may say:

- some biochemical motifs recur across domains
- shared motifs can guide hypothesis generation
- context boundaries are central to cross-domain science

At this stage we should not say:

- that all stress systems are equivalent
- that recurrence proves identical mechanism
- that human, plant, and environmental data can be merged without boundaries
