## Scope v0

This first active execution pass narrows `S4-D-R1` to:

- recurring stress and mechanism motifs across domains
- shared-vs-distinct logic between human, model, and plant/environment systems
- transferability at motif level rather than exact biomarker level

That scope is intentionally tighter than a broad multi-omics comparison review.

## Current cross-domain motif shortlist

### Tier 1 patterns

- amino-acid and energy-metabolism shifts recur across many stressed systems
- oxidative-stress and membrane-remodeling logic repeatedly appears across disease and environmental stress contexts
- shared pathway families are more transferable than exact metabolite rankings

### Tier 2 patterns

- context-specific directionality can differ even when the same pathway family recurs
- plant stress and human disease can share response motifs without being biologically equivalent
- large-scale association atlases are useful for finding recurrence, but not sufficient alone for mechanism transfer

## Why these patterns matter

They jointly show three things:

- `S4-D` can become the real bridge lane inside `SPHERE I`
- transferable mechanism work should operate on motif panels, not one-biomarker claims
- explicit context limits are part of the scientific value, not a weakness

## Practical interpretation

The current evidence already supports three real outputs:

1. a cross-domain motif evidence table

## Current working logic

### 1. Shared signals exist, but they are usually pathway-family level

The human atlas and multi-condition comparison studies show that recurrence is strongest at the level of pathway classes, lipid groups, amino-acid metabolism, and broad metabolic response structure.

### 2. Cross-study and cross-domain comparison is strongest when “shared” and “distinct” are both recorded

The AD/PSP comparison and depression clinical-vs-animal review both show that the useful layer is not generic overlap, but disciplined separation of common vs context-specific patterns.

### 3. Plant and environmental stress studies reveal transferable stress metabolism motifs

Cold, heavy-metal, and combined heat-drought studies repeatedly show carbohydrate, amino-acid, redox, and secondary-metabolite shifts that can be treated as broader stress-response logic.

### 4. Transfer must stay motif-aware, not equivalence-driven

The value is in identifying recurring response architecture, not in claiming that one system is just another version of a different one.

## Current discipline

At this stage we may say:

- some biochemical response motifs recur across domains
- motif-level transfer can organize mechanism thinking across `S1-S6`
- cross-domain interpretation becomes defensible when context limits are explicit

At this stage we should not say:

- that one plant stress profile validates a human disease profile
- that repeated pathway names guarantee shared causality
- that cross-domain similarity removes the need for domain-specific biology
