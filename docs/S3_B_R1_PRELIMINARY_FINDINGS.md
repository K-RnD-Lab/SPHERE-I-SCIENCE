## Current intervention shortlist

### Tier 1

- consortium PGPR field inoculation
- context-matched stress-adapted formulations
- co-application with organic fertilizer or reduced mineral input

### Tier 2

- carrier-optimized / biofilm-oriented delivery logic
- biofertilizer support under brackish or saline stress
- physiology-linked microbial inoculant response panels

## Why these patterns matter

They jointly show three things:

- field efficacy exists, but it is not universal
- delivery logic matters almost as much as microbial identity
- context fit often determines whether the intervention looks strong, weak, or inconsistent

## Practical interpretation

The current evidence already supports three real outputs:

1. a normalized intervention evidence table
2. a shortlist of higher-confidence validation patterns
3. a first field-facing report on where microbial biofertilizers are most plausible

## Current working logic

### 1. Consortia often outperform single strains

The teff field study supports that a PGPR consortium can outperform individual strains for yield and nutrient-uptake outcomes.

### 2. Formulation and delivery are not secondary details

The carrier and formulation review, along with the wheat formulation study, support that free-cell logic alone is often too weak for real deployment.

### 3. Co-application can be strong, but not infinitely scalable

The grassland study supports that microbial plus organic combinations can outperform controls, but also shows threshold effects when dosage becomes excessive.

### 4. Stress context changes the result

The cotton irrigation study supports that biofertilizer benefit remains meaningful under fresh and brackish conditions but weakens under stronger saline stress.

## Current discipline

At this stage we may say:

- microbial biofertilizers can show meaningful field benefit
- consortia, formulation quality, and co-application logic are recurring success factors
- efficacy is clearly conditional on crop, stress, and management context

At this stage we should not say:

- that one inoculant logic is best for all crops
- that the current shortlist is deployment-ready across settings
- that review-backed plausibility equals field-proofed performance
