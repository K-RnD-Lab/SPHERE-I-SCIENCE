# S5-B-R1 Preliminary Findings

## Research home

- Sphere: `S5`
- Lane: `S5-B Cognitive Aging & Vulnerability`
- Research line: `S5-B-R1 Cognitive Aging Risk Profiles`

## Scope v0

This first active execution pass narrows `S5-B-R1` to:

- multidomain vulnerability profiling
- cognitive risk vs resilience interpretation
- practical separation between risk factors, biomarkers, and reserve signals

## Current vulnerability shortlist

### Tier 1 patterns

- cognitive aging is heterogeneous and needs profile groups rather than one decline curve
- vascular and metabolic risk appear repeatedly as relevant vulnerability layers
- brain-age and epigenetic-age measures are promising but need interpretation discipline

### Tier 2 patterns

- modifiable risk factors matter, but their evidence strength differs
- reserve and education can change the relationship between pathology and performance
- biomarker panels are stronger when paired with cognitive endpoints

## Why these patterns matter

They jointly show three things:

- `S5-B` should become a profile-building lane, not a vague aging topic
- vulnerability logic should combine biological, cognitive, and context variables
- profile outputs are useful before any clinical prediction claim

## Practical interpretation

The current evidence already supports three real outputs:

1. a cognitive-aging vulnerability schema
2. a marker-readiness and risk-domain table
3. a first profile note separating risk, reserve, and biomarker layers

## Current working logic

### 1. Cognitive aging is not one path

Current reviews emphasize multidimensional cognitive, biological, and environmental influences.

### 2. Brain aging markers are useful but not standalone answers

Brain-age and epigenetic-clock sources are useful for risk framing but require validation and context.

### 3. Vascular and modifiable factors matter for practical interpretation

Microvascular dysfunction and modifiable risk-factor reviews strengthen the human-facing usefulness of the lane.

## Current discipline

At this stage we may say:

- multidomain vulnerability profiles are a useful research output
- cognitive aging should be interpreted through risk, reserve, and biomarker context
- profile design can support later longitudinal risk work

At this stage we should not say:

- that any single biomarker gives a complete cognitive-aging diagnosis
- that association evidence proves reversibility
- that one profile generalizes across all populations

## Immediate next step

The next execution move for `S5-B-R1` should be:

- expand the evidence table with more cohort and cognitive-domain studies
- define two to four practical profile groups
- avoid overloading the pack with generic dementia material
