## Current resilience shortlist

### Tier 1 patterns

- cognitive reserve can moderate the relationship between pathology markers and observed cognition
- resilience to AD pathology is a distinct research construct, not simply absence of disease
- successful cognitive aging needs multimodal markers, not one protective factor

### Tier 2 patterns

- education and lifestyle proxies are useful but not direct mechanisms
- resilience and resistance should be separated when possible
- intervention claims need stronger endpoints than cross-sectional association

## Why these patterns matter

- `S5-D` gives `S5` a positive biological framing

## Current working logic

### 1. Resilience is not simply low risk

Resilience can mean preserved function despite measurable pathology or risk burden.

### 2. Cognitive reserve is useful but methodologically messy

Reserve is often measured through proxies, residual models, or neurobiological indicators, so definitions must be explicit.

### 3. Support factors need readiness labels

Education, lifestyle, social, and biological support factors should be ranked by evidence role, not treated as guaranteed protection.

## Current discipline

At this stage we may say:

- resilience and reserve are useful interpretive layers
- preserved cognition under pathology deserves explicit modeling
- support-factor panels can guide research hypotheses

At this stage we should not say:

- that resilience can be guaranteed
- that one factor protects every person
- that observational associations prove intervention effects
