## Scope v0

This first active execution pass narrows `S5-E-R1` to:

- longitudinal risk markers
- incident dementia and cognitive decline endpoints
- trajectory-aware interpretation
- practical readiness limits

## Current longitudinal shortlist

### Tier 1 patterns

- plasma protein panels can predict future dementia risk years before diagnosis
- GFAP, NfL, p-tau217, and related markers repeatedly appear in risk-facing evidence
- trajectory modeling helps separate stable cognition from faster decline groups

### Tier 2 patterns

- organ-specific aging clocks can improve risk stratification but need careful translation
- epigenetic clocks are promising but not a simple replacement for cognitive and imaging measures
- marker combinations are more practical than single-marker stories

## Why these patterns matter

They jointly show three things:

- `S5-E` is the forward-looking layer of the sphere
- longitudinal evidence must be structured by time horizon and endpoint
- biomarker promise needs readiness labels before practical use

## Practical interpretation

The current evidence already supports three real outputs:

1. a longitudinal risk marker evidence table
2. a marker time-horizon panel
3. a trajectory-aware note separating prediction from diagnosis

## Current working logic

### 1. Future-risk studies are not the same as diagnostic studies

The proteomic and blood-biomarker studies support risk stratification, but their outputs must be labeled as incident-risk evidence unless clinical diagnostic validation is explicit.

### 2. Panels are stronger than single markers

Combination markers such as p-tau217 with GFAP or NfL are more useful for panel logic than isolated marker claims.

### 3. Trajectories matter

Trajectory studies show that cognitive aging is better represented as multiple paths over time rather than one average decline curve.

## Current discipline

At this stage we may say:

- longitudinal blood and aging-clock markers can support risk-panel design
- trajectory-aware interpretation is central to `S5-E`
- the evidence is strong enough for research panels, not broad self-screening claims

At this stage we should not say:

- that one biomarker test diagnoses future dementia
- that one model is ready for all populations
- that prediction accuracy removes the need for clinical context

## Immediate next step

The next execution move for `S5-E-R1` should be:

- add more longitudinal cohort records
- define a minimal marker-readiness scoring layer
- separate `early risk`, `diagnostic support`, and `progression monitoring`
