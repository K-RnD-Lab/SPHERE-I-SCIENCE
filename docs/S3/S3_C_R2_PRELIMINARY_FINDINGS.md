## Scope v0

This first active execution pass narrows `S3-C-R2` to:

- post-stress recovery
- priming and recurrent-stress response
- yield stability and reproductive resilience

That scope is intentionally tighter than a broad climate-resilience discussion.

## Current resilience shortlist

### Tier 1 patterns

- yield resilience under recurrent stress depends on genotype and timing
- recovery after rewatering does not automatically predict highest yield
- reproductive-stage protection is one of the strongest resilience signals
- stress memory and priming can matter, but their benefit is conditional

### Tier 2 patterns

- compensatory allocation after heat stress
- limited-transpiration or water-conservation traits
- stage-aware priming effects
- distinction between vigor, recovery, and resilience

## Why these patterns matter

They jointly show three things:

- crops can recover physiologically without fully protecting yield
- resilience is dynamic and stage-sensitive
- repeated or combined stress changes what counts as a useful support factor

## Current working logic

### 1. Recovery is not the same as high agricultural performance

The barley rewatering study shows that lines with strong recovery metrics do not automatically deliver the highest yield outcomes.

### 2. Priming can help, but not uniformly

The wheat recurrent-stress and drought-priming studies support that priming benefits depend on genotype, priming stage, and the later stress context.

### 3. Reproductive-stage resilience is a high-value outcome

The crop reproductive resilience review and maize field evidence both support the idea that protecting later-stage yield processes matters more than generic tolerance language.

### 4. Field interpretation must remain stricter than controlled-environment optimism

The barley field-vs-controlled comparison shows that stress-response patterns can diverge and compensatory strategies matter in field-like conditions.

## Current discipline

At this stage we may say:

- resilience should be interpreted through recovery, compensation, and yield stability together
- priming and stress memory are plausible but conditional resilience mechanisms
- reproductive-stage protection is one of the strongest applied targets

At this stage we should not say:

- that one recovery metric defines resilience
- that priming broadly solves climate stress
- that controlled-condition gains equal field-ready resilience
