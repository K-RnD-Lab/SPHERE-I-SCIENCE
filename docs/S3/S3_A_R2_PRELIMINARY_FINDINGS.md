# S3-A-R2 Preliminary Findings

## Research home

- Sphere: `S3`
- Lane: `S3-A Soil & Rhizosphere Microbiomes`
- Research line: `S3-A-R2 Soil Microbiome State Classification`

## Scope v0

This first active execution pass narrows `S3-A-R2` to:

- healthy vs stressed soil-state logic
- management-sensitive microbiome interpretation
- response-sensitive states for microbial interventions

That scope is intentionally narrower than a full soil-health platform.

Without this narrowing, `S3-A-R2` would drift into a loose collection of microbiome buzzwords with weak classification value.

## Current state and indicator shortlist

### Support-oriented patterns

- higher-diversity and more connected microbiome structures
- management systems associated with beneficial microbial enrichment
- microbiome contexts linked to stronger plant defense or resilience

### Stress or risk-oriented patterns

- disease-associated or imbalance-linked community shifts
- management-sensitive loss of interaction structure
- stress-linked microbial changes that weaken support functions

### Response-sensitive patterns

- microbiome signatures that help predict whether AMF inoculation or related interventions are likely to work

## Why these patterns matter

They jointly show three things:

- soil state is not captured by one variable
- management and crop context strongly shape interpretation
- prediction of intervention response may be more useful than generic “healthy vs unhealthy” labeling in some cases

## Practical interpretation

The current evidence already supports three real outputs:

1. a normalized microbiome-state evidence table
2. a support-vs-risk indicator panel
3. a first crop- and management-context comparison matrix

## Current working logic

### 1. Management matters as much as microbiome composition

The Communications Biology study supports that fertility source and tillage shape assembly and interaction patterns, so soil state cannot be interpreted without management context.

### 2. Support-state claims need ecological evidence

The npj Sustainable Agriculture paper supports that more supportive soil-management systems can shift microbiomes in ways linked to crop defense, not only diversity.

### 3. Generalizability is limited

The Water Research / ScienceDirect paper on healthy rhizosphere indicators supports that markers often fail to generalize cleanly across cropping and management systems.

### 4. Prediction is a valuable state class

The AMF response-prediction paper supports that some microbiome states are best interpreted as response-sensitive, not simply “healthy” or “bad.”

## Current discipline

At this stage we may say:

- microbiome states are shaped by stress and management
- support, risk, and response-sensitive classes are more defensible than one binary label
- indicator generalizability must be treated cautiously

At this stage we should not say:

- that one healthy-soil microbiome signature applies across crops
- that diversity alone is enough to classify state
- that the current framework is ready for universal routine deployment

## Immediate next step

The next execution move for `S3-A-R2` should be:

- expand the evidence table with a few more crop and disease contexts
- keep only records that sharpen classification logic
- avoid bloating the table with generic microbiome summaries
